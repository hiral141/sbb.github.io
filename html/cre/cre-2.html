Title of the Experiment : RATE EQUATION FOR METHYL ACETATE

Practical Significance:

The rate of a chemical reaction is, perhaps, its most important property because it dictates whether a reaction can occur during a lifetime. Knowing the rate law, an expression relating the rate to the concentrations of reactants, can help a chemist adjust the reaction conditions to get a more suitable rate. If there are two competing reactions for a single reagent, one can, knowing the rate law, favour the exclusive formation of a single product.

Practical Outcome

To find the rate equation for the reaction

Theory

For an irreversible uni molecular 1" order type reaction like A  Pdts., the rate equation may be written as -r A=DCA/dt = k.CA or -InCA/CA0= kt. Therefore, a plot of -InCA/CA0 vs t gives a straight line of slope k. The hydrolysis of methyl acetate is catalysed by H ions. Hydrochloric acid merely acts as a catalyst. The course of the reaction is followed by withdrawing a definite quantity of the reaction mixture after different intervals of time and titrating against a std. alkali. The amount of the alkali used is equivalent to the total amount of hydrochloric acid originally present and the amount of acetic acid formed in the reaction. Since the amount of HCI present originally is known or can be determined by titrating against the same alkali at the commencement of reaction, the amount of acetic acid formed (x) after different intervals of time(t), can be obtained. The amount of acetic acid formed at the end of the reaction is equivalent to the initial concentration CA of the ester. Suppose the volumes of the std. solution of NaOH required for the neutralization of 5ml of the reacting solution, at the commencement of the reaction, after time t and at the end of the reaction are V0, Vt, and Va respectively. Then x the amount of acetic acid formed after time t is proportional to Vt -Vo while the initial concentration of the ester is proportional to V∞-V0. Therefore, CA the concentration of the ester present after time t is proportional to
(V∞-V0) - (Vt -Vo) or V∞ - Vt.

Procedure -

  1. In a 250ml dry stoppered bottle, 100ml 0.5N HCI is taken.
  2. It is then placed in a thermostat.
  3. Stoppered bottle containing methyl acetate is also placed in a thermostat.
  4. When the two liquids have the temperature of the bath, 5ml of methyl acetate is pipetted out into the HCI and shaken well.
  5. Immediately 5ml of dry solution is withdrawn by means of dry pipette into about 25ml of ice-cold water to inert the reaction.
  6. A stop watch is started when half the liquid in the pipette is drained the cold mixture is titrated against carbonate free 0.1N NaOH.
  7. Simultaneously 5ml solution is titrated against NaOH at intervals of 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 and 60 minutes.
  8. The final titration is done when the reaction is completed i.e. after a couple of days. Graph is plotted.
