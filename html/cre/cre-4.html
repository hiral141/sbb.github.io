Title of the Experiment : DETERMINATION OF ORDER OF REACTION PART - 1

Practical Significance:

The order of a reaction tells us how the rate of reaction is affected by the concentration of the reactants.
For a zero-order reaction, the rate of reaction is independent of the concentration of reactants, so changing the reactant concentration will have no effect on the reaction rate. For a first-order reaction, the rate of reaction is directly proportional to the concentration of reactants, so doubling the reactant concentration doubles the reaction rate. For a second-order reaction, the rate of reaction is proportional to the square of the concentration of reactants, so doubling the reactant concentration quadruples the reactionrate.

Practical Outcome

To determine the order of a reaction between hydrogen peroxide and hydrogen iodide.
Part-1 To determine the order with respect to H2O2

Theory

The rate of formation of iodine in the reaction
H2O2 + 2HI = 2H2O + l2
is given by
Rate = k [ H2O2] m [HI]n
Where k is a constant at a given temperature and m and n represent the order of reaction with respect to two reactants.
The initial rate is determined by allowing the reaction tp proceed in the presence of a known (small) amount of thiosulfate and timing the interval which elapses before this is used up, ie, before iodine appears. The reciprocal of this time interval is a measure of the rate



Chemicals -

  1. 500 ml 1 N sulphuric acid,
  2. 500 ml M / 10 K L,
  3. 100 ml N / 100 Na2S2O3
  4. 50 ml of 1 volume hydrogen peroxide, and
  5. fresh starch.

Apparatus -
  1. Pipettes (5,10,20 and 25 ml);
  2. burette,
  3. conical flask,
  4. stop clock.

Procedure -

  1. In to a conical flask are pipetted 25 ml of (1 N) H2SO4 25 ml M/10 KI solution and 5 ml of N / 100 Na2S2O3 solution.
  2. A few drops of starch solution are added to the mixture and 2 ml of 1 vol H2O2 is run in from a burette, a stop clock being started when half the peroxide has been added.
  3. The time, which elapses before a blue colour appears, is noted.
  4. A series of measurements are taken using different volumes (1 - 5ml) of H2O2
